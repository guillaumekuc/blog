## Vilja

This month I wrote the synopsis for a project that I am now naming **Vilja**. The puzzle pieces were already there. The writing is what tied them together.

Vilja is a would-be game trying to teach music through gameplay as a trojan horse, hidden in proper world-building, story, and visuals. The vision as it stands would reuse and polish the gameplay explorations I've been doing around relative harmony, take advantage of the procedural 3D capability I'm acquiring through Houdini, present a 2D/3D visual style imbued with digital painting language, and assemble everything in Unity.

The world-building sits within the genre of science fantasy, under a post-peak, decaying technological landscape. The magic system (Melodurgy) proposes mind control through music. There is also an element of political tragedy at city-state scale. The story centers around three melodurges trained together, broken apart, in a struggle for governance. The tone is sometimes tender, regularly dreadful, often elegiac. The story aims to explore choices and their unintended consequences, the ethics of consent, and the elusivity of clean power.

The project's seed is inspired by references as diverse as Dan Simmons' Carrion Comfort magic system, Dune's Bene Gesserit Voice, Disco Elysium's 2D/3D art style, Ghibli's world-building and memorable characters, Arcane's character-centric storytelling, J. Jay Berthume's music theory teachings, Orwell's 1984, Michael Levin's real-world biology research alongside Jean-Marc Jancovici's ecological prospective, and personal experience.

I intend to dedicate the following month to building-up the case for why such a project should get built further — through pitch-deck writing, concept art and key shots painting, as well as bounded technical demos. This is a lot of what I began doing already this month, but I will present it next month.

# Retrospective

The past few months following my departure from Visioglobe have been rich in exploration, which is something to be expected after 3.5 years of exploitation in the context of an interactive indoor mapping SaaS.

During this time I redesigned and web-developed my portfolio website using modern tools like Figma and Cursor. I built a personal digital infrastructure using a VPS and services like Syncthing, Perforce, and Nextcloud. Even OpenClaw (AI assistant). I produced several music pieces using both pure MIDI workflows and recording techniques in Reaper, and I developed Chordex — a tool that maps all possible chord relationships in the tertian system to specific emotional colors — alongside a gameplay prototype for a relative harmony trainer. I got my drawing skills warm again and produced some digital paintings. I also produced Blender 3D work tied to real-world use cases, and got myself familiar with Houdini, unlocking procedural modeling as a new available tool. I tried out many production 3D tools in an exercise to set up a pipeline for a potential inbound VR project in Unity.

Today, my set of skills feels warm again: I am mostly live across digital art, 3D, music, and development. Periods of exploitation call for periods of exploration as a release valve. Periods of exploration call for periods of exploitation as a crystallizing and stabilizing act. In my case, that now means integration. I was in need of a flagship project capable of absorbing the full scope of my practice — a project where I can express Craft, Industrial, Scientific, and Expressive ethoi all at once. That project appears to be Vilja.

## About AI

I recently wrote a blog article about AI as an attempt to gauge the situation clear-eyed and to use the learning to properly orient myself moving forward. The article framed the current changes as a re-weighting of the workplace away from a craft ethos and towards more of an industrial ethos. It does not conclude that a Craft ethos has become irrelevant, especially from a signaling standpoint, nor that the Industrial ethos alone is sufficient. It also reflects on the continued or growing relevance of the Scientific ethos and the Expressive ethos.
